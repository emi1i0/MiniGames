# Ciudad de Juguete

The art direction for Skyline's Three.js renderer. Every visual decision — geometry, material, light, the sky that changes with altitude — answers to this document.

Skyline is a toy on a table, not a photograph of a city. The fantasy is the one a kid has stacking wooden blocks: fat, rounded apartment floors with a satisfying heft, snapped one on top of another until the tower wobbles and you hold your breath. Everything is built to feel **handled** — pieces you could pick up, painted in flat happy enamel, wearing a soft ink outline like a sticker. Nothing here is grim, weathered, or realistic. If a surface looks like it belongs in an architectural render, it is wrong; it should look like it belongs in a playroom.

Form is chunky and eased, never inflated and never sharp. A floor is a wide rounded box a little squatter than it is broad, with a low bevel that catches the sun — read as *solid*, a thing with weight that lands with a thud. The windows are simple lit rectangles indexed in a tidy grid, warm gold squares punched into the enamel; some lit, some dark, so the building reads as *inhabited* the moment it's placed (that is the whole point — these are habitantes, not points). Each building is its own candy color, so a finished tower is a stack of distinct colored buildings and the switch to a new hue is the reward for completing one. Every mesh carries the inverted-hull ink outline so the silhouette stays crisp against any sky.

Color is saturated and warm, chosen so a stack of floors looks like a sunlit toy skyline. Each building gets its own candy enamel — coral, mint, butter, sky, lilac — flat cel bands, no gradients pretending to be light. The one bright voice reserved from that palette is **gold**: lit windows, the perfect-combo sparkle, the population tally. Gold means *life and reward*, and nothing else gets to be gold. The crane is honest machinery — a warm painted jib and a taut cable — never the star of the frame; the block hanging from it is.

Light behaves like a clear afternoon: one warm key sun casting a soft real shadow down the side of the tower, a cool sky fill so the shaded faces don't go black, and just enough bloom that the gold windows and the combo sparks *bleed* without the whole scene glowing. The shadow is not decoration — it is how the player feels the tower lean. When the center of mass drifts, the whole stack tilts and the shadow swings with it; that swing is the warning, earned by the physics, not painted on.

Altitude is the story, told by the sky. You start at street level among little toy houses under a pastel morning blue; as the tower climbs the world answers — the blue deepens, clouds drift past and thin out, the air goes to cold stratosphere indigo, and finally the sky falls away to black speckled with stars. The transition is a slow honest lerp keyed to the tower's height, never a hard cut, so the player *feels* themselves leaving the ground. The higher you are, the lonelier and quieter the frame gets — the reward for a tall building is a view no one else has.

The camera sits almost in profile, a hair to the side and above, far enough back that perspective is gentle — because this is a game of timing, and the player must read the swinging block crossing the swaying tower cleanly, at a glance, sixty times a second. Depth exists to make the toy feel real, never to obscure the one alignment that matters. Ornament that competes with that read — busy backgrounds, loud particles, anything that muddies where the block is going to land — is removed, however cute. What remains should look like the best toy on the shelf: simple, warm, inviting, and just precarious enough that you can't put it down.
